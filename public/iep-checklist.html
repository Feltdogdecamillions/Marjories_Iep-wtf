IEP Meeting Survival Checklist

Your Complete Guide to IEP Meeting Preparation

From "IEPs & WTFs: A Parent's Survival Guide to Special Education" by Marjorie Stevens

Welcome! This checklist is designed to help you prepare for, participate in, and follow up after your child's IEP meeting. Print this out, bring it with you, and use it as your roadmap to effective advocacy. Remember: you are an equal member of the IEP team, and your voice matters.

1 Before the Meeting

Review & Organize Documents

Review your child's current IEP and note what's working and what isn't
Read all evaluation reports and assessments thoroughly
Review progress reports and report cards from this school year
Organize any outside evaluations or medical documentation you want to share
Gather work samples that show your child's strengths and challenges

Prepare Your Observations & Concerns

Write down specific examples of your child's progress and struggles
Document any behavioral or emotional concerns you've observed
Note your child's strengths and interests to build on
List any accommodations or services you want to request or discuss
Prepare questions about anything in the reports you don't understand

Logistics & Support

Confirm the meeting date, time, and location
Ask who will be attending the meeting and their roles
Consider bringing a support person (spouse, advocate, friend) to take notes
Request an interpreter if needed (language or sign language)
Bring this checklist, paper/pen, and all relevant documents in an organized folder
💡 Pro Tip:

Send your concerns and questions to the team in writing 3-5 days before the meeting. This gives everyone time to prepare thoughtful responses and shows you're an engaged, organized parent.

2 During the Meeting

Understand What's Being Discussed

Ask for clarification if you don't understand terminology or acronyms
Request explanations of test scores and what they mean in practical terms
Ask "What does this look like in the classroom?" for any abstract concepts
Take notes or have someone take notes for you

Essential Questions to Ask

"How will we measure progress toward these goals?" (Be specific about data collection)
"What does this accommodation/service look like in practice?"
"How often will this service be provided, and by whom?"
"What happens if my child isn't making adequate progress?"
"How will you communicate progress to me throughout the year?"
"What support/training will teachers receive to implement these accommodations?"

Advocate Effectively

Share your observations from home - you see things teachers don't
Be specific when requesting services ("I'd like speech therapy 2x/week for 30 minutes")
If you disagree, say so respectfully and explain why
Don't feel pressured to sign anything you're not comfortable with
Request a break if you feel overwhelmed or need time to think
🛡️ Remember Your Rights:

You have the right to disagree with the IEP, request additional evaluations, bring an advocate, record the meeting (check state laws), and request another meeting. You are an equal member of this team.

3 Key IEP Components to Review

Present Levels (PLAAFP): Does this accurately describe your child's current abilities and needs?
Annual Goals: Are they specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART)?
Accommodations: Will these genuinely help your child access the curriculum?
Services: Type, frequency, duration, and location are all clearly specified
Placement (LRE): Is this the least restrictive environment where your child can succeed?
Transition Planning: (If age 14+) Are post-secondary goals and transition services included?
Progress Reporting: How and how often will you receive updates?

4 After the Meeting

Review the draft IEP document carefully before signing
Compare the written IEP to what was discussed in the meeting
If you need time to review, don't sign yet - you can take it home
Keep a copy of the signed IEP in your records
Send a thank you email to the team, summarizing key points discussed
Share relevant parts of the IEP with your child (age-appropriate)
Set reminders to check in on progress throughout the year
Document any concerns or wins as they happen during the year
📧 Follow-Up Email Template:

"Thank you for meeting with me today. I appreciate the team's time and collaboration. To confirm my understanding: [list key decisions, services agreed upon, and next steps]. Please let me know if I've missed anything. I look forward to working together to support [child's name]."

⚠️ Red Flags - When to Push Back

The team pressures you to sign immediately without time to review
Goals are vague or not measurable ("will improve reading skills")
You're told "we don't do that here" or "that's not how we typically do things"
Services are reduced without clear data showing progress
The IEP seems predetermined - your input isn't genuinely considered
Budget or staffing is mentioned as a reason services can't be provided
You're made to feel like a difficult or demanding parent for advocating
